# Discussion

This comprehensive meta-analysis of five randomized controlled trials demonstrates that adding immune checkpoint inhibitors to neoadjuvant chemotherapy provides statistically significant and clinically meaningful benefits for patients with triple-negative breast cancer. The consistency of benefit across pathologic complete response, event-free survival, and overall survival—combined with a manageable safety profile—supports current guideline recommendations for routine use of ICI plus chemotherapy in this setting.

## Principal Findings

Our analysis yields several key findings. First, ICI plus chemotherapy improved pCR rates by 26% relative risk (absolute benefit 13.8%, NNT=7), with remarkably low heterogeneity (I²=0%) despite differences in ICI agents, chemotherapy regimens, and patient populations. This consistency suggests a robust class effect across PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors. Second, the pCR improvement translated to durable survival benefits: 34% reduction in recurrence or death (EFS HR 0.66, absolute 5-year benefit 9.2%, NNT=11) and concordant overall survival improvements in both trials with mature data (pooled 5-year absolute benefit 9.3%, NNT=11). Third, PD-L1 expression status did not predict differential treatment benefit (interaction p=0.169), with both PD-L1-positive and PD-L1-negative patients deriving clinically relevant improvements in pCR. Fourth, while ICI therapy increased serious adverse events (RR 1.50, NNH=10) and grade 3+ immune-related adverse events (~13%, NNH=9), fatal events remained rare (0.40%), and most toxicities were reversible with established management protocols.

## Validation of pCR as Surrogate Endpoint

A critical finding is the concordance between pCR and long-term survival outcomes, validating pCR as a surrogate endpoint for EFS and OS in neoadjuvant TNBC trials using immunotherapy. The near-identical absolute benefits for EFS and OS (9.2% vs 9.3% at 5 years, both NNT=11) demonstrate that pathologic tumor eradication translates directly to prevention of cancer mortality. This aligns with FDA's acceptance of pCR for accelerated approval in high-risk early breast cancer and supports the use of pCR as a primary endpoint in future neoadjuvant ICI trials. The consistency of this relationship across all three survival-reporting trials, despite different ICI agents and follow-up durations, strengthens confidence in pCR-based regulatory decisions.

## PD-L1 as Prognostic, Not Predictive

Our subgroup analysis clarifies the controversial role of PD-L1 expression. While PD-L1-positive patients had higher baseline pCR rates (consistent with PD-L1 as a favorable prognostic marker), the lack of significant treatment-by-biomarker interaction (p=0.169) indicates PD-L1 does not identify patients who differentially benefit from ICI. Notably, KEYNOTE-522's EFS data showed numerically greater benefit in PD-L1-negative patients (HR 0.48 vs 0.67), directly contradicting the predictive biomarker hypothesis. These findings have immediate clinical implications: PD-L1 testing should not be used to exclude patients from neoadjuvant ICI therapy. The contrasting situation in metastatic TNBC—where IMpassion130 showed benefit restricted to PD-L1-positive patients—may reflect differences in immune priming potential between neoadjuvant and metastatic settings or the enhanced antigen presentation afforded by intact lymphatic drainage prior to surgery.

## Safety and Benefit-Risk Assessment

The safety profile of neoadjuvant ICI aligns with experience in other cancer types. The 13% rate of grade 3+ immune-related adverse events is comparable to adjuvant melanoma (15–20%) and adjuvant lung cancer (10–15%) trials. Critically, the 0.40% fatal adverse event rate is within the expected range for ICI therapy (0.3–0.8% across indications) and compares favorably with chemotherapy-associated mortality. The benefit-risk calculus strongly favors ICI plus chemotherapy: the curative potential (NNT=7 for pCR, NNT=11 for 5-year survival) substantially outweighs the risk of manageable and typically reversible toxicity (NNH=9–10) and rare fatal events (NNH=250).

A practical consideration is treatment discontinuation, occurring in 27.6% of ICI-treated patients versus 14.1% of controls. Importantly, discontinuation does not negate benefits already achieved—patients who attain pCR before ICI discontinuation still experience improved EFS. Clinicians should adopt proactive monitoring strategies (baseline and serial thyroid function, liver enzymes, creatinine) and educate patients about early irAE symptoms to enable prompt intervention. Grade 2 toxicities warrant ICI interruption with consideration of corticosteroids; grade 3–4 events mandate permanent ICI discontinuation and high-dose immunosuppression. Endocrinopathies (hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency) require lifelong hormone replacement but are generally well-tolerated.

## Comparison with Other Settings

The magnitude of benefit observed in neoadjuvant TNBC exceeds that seen in most other ICI indications. The pCR improvement (RR 1.26) translates to a 13.8% absolute benefit, substantially larger than the 2–5% absolute OS benefits typically observed in metastatic solid tumors. The 5-year OS benefit (9.3%, NNT=11) compares favorably with adjuvant melanoma (HR 0.57, ~8% absolute benefit) and adjuvant lung cancer (HR 0.66–0.79, ~4% absolute benefit). This enhanced efficacy likely reflects the immunologically privileged neoadjuvant window: chemotherapy-induced tumor cell death releases neoantigens, intact lymph nodes facilitate T-cell priming, and surgical removal of the primary tumor may prevent immune exhaustion. These findings support exploration of neoadjuvant ICI strategies in other tumor types where adjuvant ICI has demonstrated benefit.

## Limitations

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, long-term survival data remain immature for three trials (IMpassion031, NeoTRIPaPDL1, CamRelief), limiting our OS meta-analysis to two trials with moderate heterogeneity. The pooled OS estimate should be interpreted cautiously given the wide Hartung-Knapp-adjusted confidence interval; however, both individual trials demonstrated significant benefits, and the absolute OS benefit (9.3%) aligns perfectly with EFS (9.2%), providing biological plausibility. Second, safety reporting was incomplete, with only one trial (KEYNOTE-522) providing detailed grade 3+ immune-related adverse event data. This limits precision of pooled safety estimates, though real-world cohorts corroborate similar toxicity rates. Third, PD-L1 definitions varied across trials (CPS≥1, IC≥1%, investigator-defined), introducing heterogeneity into subgroup analyses. A standardized PD-L1 assay and cutoff would facilitate future comparisons. Fourth, we did not access individual patient data, precluding covariate-adjusted analyses and precise time-to-event modeling. Fifth, follow-up duration varies (14–75 months), and late recurrences beyond 5 years may modify absolute benefit estimates. Finally, all trials were industry-sponsored, raising potential publication bias concerns, though trial registry searches identified no unreported negative studies.

## Clinical Implications

Our findings support several clinical recommendations. First, ICI plus chemotherapy should be considered standard of care for neoadjuvant treatment of stage II-III TNBC, as reflected in current ASCO, NCCN, and ESMO guidelines. Second, PD-L1 testing is not required for treatment selection; both PD-L1-positive and PD-L1-negative patients benefit. Third, patient selection should focus on contraindications (active autoimmune disease, solid organ transplant, prior severe irAE) rather than biomarker-driven exclusion. Fourth, multidisciplinary teams should implement proactive toxicity monitoring and management protocols. Fifth, patients should be counseled about the balance of curative benefits (1 in 7 additional pCR, 1 in 11 fewer deaths at 5 years) versus manageable risks (1 in 9–10 grade 3+ irAE, 1 in 250 fatal event).

## Future Directions

Several research priorities emerge from this analysis. First, updated OS meta-analyses should be performed as IMpassion031, NeoTRIPaPDL1, and CamRelief report mature survival data. Second, individual patient data meta-analyses would enable covariate-adjusted estimates and identification of patients at exceptionally high or low benefit. Third, biomarker studies beyond PD-L1—including tumor mutational burden, gene expression profiling, circulating tumor DNA, and immune cell phenotyping—may refine patient selection. Fourth, de-escalation trials should evaluate whether chemotherapy can be reduced or omitted in patients achieving pCR with ICI. Fifth, mechanistic studies should elucidate why PD-L1 is prognostic in neoadjuvant TNBC but predictive in metastatic disease. Sixth, cost-effectiveness analyses are needed to inform reimbursement decisions, particularly in resource-limited settings. Finally, quality-of-life assessments should quantify the patient experience of immune-related adverse events beyond clinical trial reporting.

## Conclusions

Neoadjuvant immune checkpoint inhibitors plus chemotherapy provide statistically significant and clinically meaningful improvements in pathologic complete response, event-free survival, and overall survival for patients with triple-negative breast cancer. The concordance across endpoints validates pCR as a surrogate for long-term survival, supporting regulatory pathways and accelerating drug development. Benefits are independent of PD-L1 expression status, indicating that biomarker testing should not restrict access to therapy. The favorable benefit-risk profile—with curative potential outweighing manageable and reversible toxicity—supports current guideline recommendations for routine use in neoadjuvant TNBC treatment.
