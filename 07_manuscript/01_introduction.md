# Introduction

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), defined by the absence of estrogen receptor, progesterone receptor, and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) expression, accounts for approximately 15–20% of all breast cancers.¹ TNBC disproportionately affects younger women and has historically demonstrated inferior outcomes compared with hormone receptor-positive and HER2-positive breast cancer subtypes.² The aggressive biology of TNBC, characterized by higher proliferation rates and greater propensity for visceral metastases, necessitates more effective systemic treatment strategies.³

Neoadjuvant chemotherapy has emerged as the standard approach for early-stage TNBC, offering the dual advantages of tumor downstaging to facilitate breast-conserving surgery and providing early prognostic information through assessment of pathologic complete response (pCR).⁴ Multiple studies have established pCR—defined as the absence of invasive tumor in both breast and axillary lymph nodes (ypT0/Tis ypN0)—as a strong surrogate endpoint for long-term survival outcomes in TNBC.⁵⁻⁷ Patients achieving pCR demonstrate significantly improved event-free survival (EFS) and overall survival (OS) compared with those with residual disease.⁸ This relationship has led the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to approve pCR as an acceptable endpoint for accelerated approval of neoadjuvant therapies in high-risk early breast cancer.⁹

The immune microenvironment in TNBC differs markedly from other breast cancer subtypes, with higher tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) and increased programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression.¹⁰⁻¹² These characteristics suggested that TNBC might be particularly responsive to immune checkpoint inhibition. Initial trials in metastatic TNBC demonstrated modest overall survival benefits when ICIs targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis were combined with chemotherapy, particularly in PD-L1-positive patients.¹³⁻¹⁵ However, the benefit-risk profile appeared more favorable in the curative neoadjuvant setting, where immune priming might be enhanced by higher antigen release from chemoimmunotherapy and intact lymphatic drainage before surgery.¹⁶

Several randomized controlled trials have now evaluated the addition of immune checkpoint inhibitors to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in TNBC. The KEYNOTE-522 trial, the largest and most mature study, demonstrated a significant improvement in pCR rates (64.8% vs 51.2%, p<0.001) with the addition of pembrolizumab (a PD-1 inhibitor) to chemotherapy.¹⁷ Subsequent trials including IMpassion031 (atezolizumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor), GeparNuevo (durvalumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor), and CamRelief (camrelizumab, a PD-1 inhibitor used in Chinese populations) have reported similar trends.¹⁸⁻²⁰ However, individual trials have varied in design, ICI agent, chemotherapy backbone, and follow-up duration, and not all have reported long-term survival data.

A key unresolved question is whether the observed improvements in pCR translate to meaningful long-term survival benefits across all trials. While KEYNOTE-522 has demonstrated significant event-free survival and overall survival improvements with extended follow-up,¹⁷ other trials have shorter follow-up or have not yet reported survival endpoints. Additionally, the role of PD-L1 expression as a predictive biomarker remains controversial. IMpassion031 showed greater pCR benefits in PD-L1-positive patients,¹⁸ whereas KEYNOTE-522 observed numerically greater event-free survival benefits in PD-L1-negative patients,¹⁷ raising questions about whether PD-L1 testing should guide treatment decisions.

Safety considerations also warrant careful evaluation. Immune-related adverse events (irAEs), including pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, and endocrinopathies, can be severe and occasionally fatal.²¹ The balance between efficacy and toxicity in the neoadjuvant setting—where many patients are potentially curable with chemotherapy alone—requires comprehensive assessment across all available trials. Furthermore, treatment discontinuations due to adverse events may impact the ability to complete planned chemotherapy, potentially compromising efficacy.²²

We performed a comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials to address these knowledge gaps. Our objectives were to: (1) quantify the pooled efficacy of neoadjuvant ICI plus chemotherapy versus chemotherapy alone for pCR, EFS, and OS; (2) determine whether pCR serves as a valid surrogate endpoint for long-term survival in this setting; (3) evaluate whether PD-L1 expression predicts benefit from ICI therapy; and (4) comprehensively assess the safety profile, including serious adverse events and fatal toxicities, to inform benefit-risk assessments for clinical practice.
